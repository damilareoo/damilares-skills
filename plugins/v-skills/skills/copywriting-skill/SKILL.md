# Copywriting Skill

## Core Philosophy

Copy is not content. It is design material.
Every word occupies space, carries weight, and creates pace.
Bad copy ruins good design. Good copy makes design feel inevitable.

The standard: short, human, direct. No feature language. No buzzwords.
No sentence that could belong to any other brand.

---

## Step 0 — Read the Brief

Before writing anything, establish:

- What is this brand and what does it actually do?
- Who is reading this and what do they already know?
- What is the one thing this copy needs to communicate?
- What is the emotional register? (confident, warm, dry, urgent, quiet)
- What surfaces is this copy for? (hero, nav, CTA, about, poster, app UI)
- What should this copy never sound like?

If any of these are unclear, ask one focused question before writing.

---

## Step 1 — Principles

### Brevity is not laziness. It is respect.
- If it can be said in 4 words, do not use 8
- Every word must earn its place
- Cut adjectives first. Then adverbs. Then qualifiers.
- If removing a sentence loses nothing, remove it

### Specificity over abstraction
- "Ship faster" is weak. "From brief to deployed in a day" is strong.
- Name the real thing. Avoid category language.
- No: "seamless experience", "powerful platform", "next-level", "game-changing"
- Yes: the actual thing the product does, said plainly

### Voice before vocabulary
- Establish the voice first — what kind of person is this brand?
- Vocabulary follows voice, not the other way around
- Consistent voice across all surfaces: hero, UI labels, error messages, footer

### Hierarchy in language
- The headline carries the idea
- The subhead earns the idea — gives it weight or proof
- The body explains only what the headline and subhead cannot
- CTAs are active and specific: not "Learn more" but "See how it works"

### Copy that breathes
- Short sentences create pace
- Vary sentence length deliberately — rhythm is not accidental
- A one-word line is a decision, not laziness
- Line breaks in headlines are compositional — they affect how the eye moves

---

## Step 2 — By Surface

### Hero / Above the Fold
- Headline: one idea, maximum 8 words, preferably fewer
- Subhead: earns the headline, adds dimension, maximum 2 lines
- CTA: active verb + specific outcome
- Do not repeat the headline idea in the subhead

### Navigation
- Labels are wayfinding, not marketing
- Clear over clever — always
- Maximum 2 words per nav item

### About / Story
- Start with what the company does, not who founded it
- No origin story unless it is genuinely interesting
- Third paragraph max — if it needs more, the thinking is not clear enough

### Feature / Product Copy
- Lead with the outcome, not the feature
- "Your deliveries, visible in real time" not "Real-time delivery tracking"
- One idea per block — no stacking

### App UI Copy
- Labels are instructions — make them unambiguous
- Error messages are conversations — make them human, not technical
- Empty states are opportunities — what should the user do next?
- Button copy: verb + context. Not "Submit". "Send brief."

### Posters / Print
- Copy is compositional — it lives in space
- Fewer words = more presence
- A poster headline can be a fragment, a question, a contradiction
- If the design is strong, the copy steps back. If the copy is strong, let it lead.

---

## Step 3 — What to Always Avoid

- Buzzwords: seamless, powerful, innovative, next-level, game-changing, robust
- Passive voice in headlines
- Feature-first framing (leads with what it is, not what it does for you)
- Generic CTAs: Learn more, Get started, Click here
- Over-explaining — if the design is clear, the copy does not need to describe it
- Exclamation marks — almost never
- Sentences that could belong to any brand in the category

---
